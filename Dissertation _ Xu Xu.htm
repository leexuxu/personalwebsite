Dissertation

To Repress or To Co-opt? Authoritarian Control in the Age of Digital Surveillance

My dissertation book project examines how digital surveillance helps authoritarian governments optimize repression and co-optation strategies to control citizens. This question is important because the world’s autocracies are experiencing significant growth in domestic surveillance despite a reverse trend among western democracies since the 2013 global surveillance disclosures leaked by the former NSA contractor Edward Snowden. I approach this question using game theory models, causal identification strategies, original measures of repression and co-optation compiled from archival data, and in-depth interviews.

I argue that when digital surveillance enables dictators to identify their most threatening opponents, they substitute targeted repression for co-optation. In contrast, without surveillance, dictators use non-exclusive co-optation to buy off moderate opponents to prevent coordinated uprisings, especially when citizens can better communicate with each other through information technologies. I use dynamic games with incomplete information to formalize the theory. Employing difference-in-differences models based on the Golden Shield project in China and the Chinanet company’s broadband upgrade, I find strong evidence that is consistent with my arguments. My dissertation highlights the crucial role of information in dictators’ tradeoff between repression and cooptation. It implies a striking fact: when governments have an information advantage, citizens are worse off, though improvements in citizens’ information make them better off.
